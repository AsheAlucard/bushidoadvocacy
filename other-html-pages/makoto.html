Makoto

Integrity (Makoto)

Makoto is the virtue of honesty and sincerity. To the samurai, words and actions had to be aligned — falsehood or deceit destroyed trust and honor.

History & Cultural Significance

Samurai treated promises as sacred. A man of Bushidō did not need written contracts — his word was binding. Makoto meant absolute sincerity, not just avoiding lies but embodying truthfulness in all dealings.

Integrity was seen as the glue that held together relationships of trust between lords, vassals, and allies.

Example in Practice

Keeping a dangerous vow even when it brings hardship, or admitting one’s mistakes openly, were considered acts of Makoto.

Practical Lesson

Makoto in daily life means being dependable, truthful, and transparent. It builds the trust that makes relationships and communities thrive.

"Bushidō is realized in the presence of death. This means choosing death whenever there is a choice between life and death. There is no other reasoning." — Yamamoto Tsunetomo, Hagakure
